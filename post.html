Perspective 1

"We should spread awareness of the issue and encourage governments worldwide to create and enforce legislation that mandates companies that rely on encrypted data to begin to update to the new algorithms." Those who fall in this category staunchly believe that we must be prepared for the advent of quantum computing as an imminent threat and must be dealt with as swiftly as possible. If enough of a commotion is made, governments must take notice, and many will likely heed the call to ensure that data remains secure. Further, governments have their own interests to protect, as all governments (probably) use encryption to send and store confidential information.

Perspective 2

We should let the future unfold on its own. Quantum-resistant algorithms do already exist, and due to privacy being in nearly everyoneâ€™s best interest, they will be gradually adopted on their own, in time (Mohseni, et al.). Furthermore, in order for companies to gain the public trust, it may be necessary for them to update their encryptions in order to retain customers and keep in business. Additionally, even today, some encryption schemes that are considered breakable by the latest technology (i.e. RSA 512) are still in minimal use (Stubbs). According to this perspective, in 25 years, most data will be secure - at least secure enough that there is not much to worry about.

Perspective 3

While it does seem logical that the shift would occur naturally, many encryption algorithms are buried deep inside existing programs, and some are skeptical that they would be updated without nudging, largely due to the effort and money required to update them (Chow). Further, the widespread knowledge of this issue in academia has resulted in a large number of individuals (mathematicians, computer scientists, etc.) working to solve it, so the general public may have little to worry about.
