  • 1978 Cryptosystem Resists Quantum Attack www.technologyreview.com/s/420287/1978-cryptosystem-resists-quantum-attack/

    • This article discusses a nearly forgotten alogirthm in the crytogoraphic world that was developed in 1978. This algorithm happens to be resistant to quantum attacks, and, due to its computational complexity is being sought after so that it can be adapted for a post-quantum future. The article runs through a brief history of ecnryption algorithms, with a specific focus on asymmetric and symmetric code/algorithms and how they relate to modern ecnryption standards. The article then desribes Peter Shor's quantum algorithm that allows quantum computers to break the modern encryption standard. It then delineates why a certain algorithm from 1978 relies on a system different than the modern algorithms - it uses the hidden supgroup problem to create encryptions as opposed to factorization of integers.

  • How a quantum computer could break 2048-bit RSA encryption in 8 hours www.technologyreview.com/s/613596/how-a-quantum-computer-could-break-2048-bit-rsa-encryption-in-8-hours/

    • This article discusses the current (as of the time it was written: May 2019) state of quantum computing. Specifically, this article focuses on the development of a an algorithm that could break the modern encryption standard - 2048-bit RSA - in around 8 hours. The article first discusses current estimates for how long it will take for data encrypted with 2048-bit RSA to become unsecure (25 years). It then describes the ways that this new algorithm is more efficient than Peter Shor's algorithm (the algorithm that originally showed that quantum computers could break 2048-bit RSA). The article concludes with various reasons why this is worrisome.

  • The new benchmark quantum computers must beat to achieve quantum supremacy www.technologyreview.com/s/613507/the-new-benchmark-quantum-computers-must-beat-to-achieve-quantum-supremacy/

    • This article reports on a test that physicists have designed in order to show that a given quantum computer has achieved quantum supremacy. The article begins by explaining why a benchmark is helpful in determining the strngth of a given computer - as many are designed with specific tasks in mind. It also discusses an important issue with defining quantum supremacy relative to the power of classical computers - as the most powerful classical supercomputers are being improved upon constantly. The article concludes by contrasting theory with reality - as classical computers require more energy to have the equivalent power of a quantum computer, humans are hitting the limit of the amount of energy that can produced, giving quantum computers an ever increasing advantage.
